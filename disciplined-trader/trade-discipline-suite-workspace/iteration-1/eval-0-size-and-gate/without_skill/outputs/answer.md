A 3:1 reward-to-risk is a genuinely good setup on paper. You only need to be right ~33% of the time to break even over a series of trades like this, so the math is in your favor *if the levels are real*.

## Position sizing (the part that actually matters)
The right size is driven by how much of the account you're willing to lose if the stop hits — not by how much you "want to make."

| Risk per trade | $ at risk | Shares (risk ÷ $0.25) | Position size | % of account deployed |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1% (recommended)** | $80 | 320 | $1,344 | 17% |
| 2% (aggressive) | $160 | 640 | $2,688 | 34% |

**Recommended: 320 shares risking $80 (1% of account).**

- If stopped out: you lose ~$80.
- If target hits: you make ~$240 (3 × your risk).

I'd steer you to 1% rather than 2% here specifically because it's a *running small-cap*. Those move fast and gap, which makes the next section the real decision point.

## The honest risk warnings — read these before you commit
1. **Your stop may not fill at $3.95.** Small-caps that are running are volatile and thin. On a flush or a gap-down, you can fill well below $3.95 (slippage). Your "$80 risk" is a best-case; size as if the real risk could be 1.5–2x that. This is the single biggest reason to use 1%, not 2%.
2. **"Running" means you may be chasing.** If the stock has already moved a lot today, $4.20 might be late. Buying strength into a target only 18% away can mean entering right as early buyers take profits.
3. **$3.95 support is only as good as the volume behind it.** "Prior support that held" is a real edge only if it held on meaningful volume and the broader move is intact. A single bounce on light volume is weak.
4. **Liquidity / spread.** Check the bid-ask spread and average volume. A wide spread on a low-float name quietly eats your edge and makes both your entry and stop worse than the plan assumes.

## Verdict: Conditional GO
The structure is sound — 3:1 reward-to-risk, a defined stop at a real level, and a sane position size. **Take it, but only at 1% risk (320 shares, ~$80 at risk), and only if all of these are true:**

- [ ] The stock isn't already extended far above $4.20 (you're entering near the level, not chasing $0.30+ above it).
- [ ] $3.95 held on decent volume, and the stock is still above it now.
- [ ] The spread is tight and volume is high enough that your stop can realistically fill near $3.95.
- [ ] You will actually honor the stop — set it as a working order, don't "watch it."

If you can't check those boxes, it's a **no / wait**: skip it or wait for a pullback closer to support where your risk-per-share shrinks and your entry isn't chasing.
