# Weekly Review — Should You Size Up?

**Verdict: No. Not yet. Hold your current size (100 shares) for at least another clean week.**

The headline number looks fine — you're green for the two weeks. But sizing up is a decision about *discipline and process stability*, not about whether the P&L is positive. On that test, this stretch is a caution flag, not a green light.

On the surface: profitable, profit factor comfortably above 1, positive expectancy. If this were the only lens, you'd size up.

## Why the answer is still "no"

**1. Every dollar of edge came from your disciplined trades. The rule breaks bled it back.**

- 18 trades where you followed the plan: **+10.5R** (avg +0.58R each).
- 4 trades where you broke the plan: **−2.9R** (avg −0.73R each).

Your edge is real *only when you follow your process*. The moment you deviate, your expectancy flips negative. Adding size right now multiplies the losing behavior just as much as the winning behavior — and the losing behavior is currently active.

**2. The breaks are not random — they share one signal.**

All four rule breaks carry the same `deviation_signal`: **`urge-to-make-it-back`**. The specific breaks were: oversizing (T03), chasing an extended entry (T09), moving a stop (T14), and taking a no-catalyst trade (T19). Different mechanics, identical root cause — revenge/recovery impulse after a red trade. That's a tilt pattern, and it's the single most dangerous thing to scale into. Sizing up amplifies exactly the emotional state you haven't yet brought under control.

You'd be sizing up into a cooling streak, not a hot one. The right time to add risk is when process quality is *rising*, not when results are decaying and breaks are recurring.

## What "ready to size up" would actually look like

Set an objective gate so the decision isn't emotional:

- **Zero rule breaks** across a full week (the discipline metric matters more than P&L here).
- That clean week is also **profitable / positive expectancy** on plan-followed trades.
- No `urge-to-make-it-back` deviation signals logged at all.

Hit all three, and you've earned a step up (e.g. 100 → 150 shares, not a double). Until then, the constraint isn't your strategy — it's the revenge-trading impulse, and more size makes that more expensive, not less.

## Bottom line

The strategy works when you do. Keep size flat, fix the `urge-to-make-it-back` tilt, log a clean week, then size up.
